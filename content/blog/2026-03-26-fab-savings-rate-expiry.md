For the past several months, First Abu Dhabi Bank has been offering a promotional savings rate of 4%. For UAE savers who wanted to park cash without locking it up, it was the easiest answer in the market.

That rate expires March 31, 2026.

After the promotion ends, FAB's standard flexible savings rate drops to around 3.25%. If you've been relying on that account as your primary savings vehicle, your yield just got cut by 18%.

## What the UAE flexible savings market looks like now

Here's where things stand for savers who want liquidity — no lock-in, no fixed deposit, ability to withdraw anytime:

| Option | Rate | Condition |
|---|---|---|
| FAB (post-promo) | ~3.25% | No condition |
| Sarwa Save | ~3.2% net | 0.5% annual fee deducted |
| Wio Bank flexible | 3.25% | No condition |
| Wio Bank (full rate) | 6% | Salary routing required |
| Mashreq Neo | 6.25% | Salary routing required |
| UAE banks (standard) | 0.5–2.5% | No condition |
| Vault | ~5.4% | No condition, no fee |

The salary-routing rates from Wio and Mashreq are genuinely good, but they require your employer to route your salary there. Not everyone has that option, and switching salary banks involves paperwork that many UAE residents — particularly expats with international employers — cannot easily do.

The no-condition, no-lock-in tier is now effectively led by Vault.

## Why FAB's rate dropped

The promotional rate was never sustainable at the product level — it was a customer acquisition tool, offering rates above what the underlying product economics support at current interest rate levels.

UAE bank rates generally track the Central Bank of the UAE's base rate, which follows the US Federal Reserve. After 75 basis points of Fed cuts in 2025, the CBUAE base rate is now 3.65%. Standard bank savings products — which park your money in short-duration instruments — are not going to sustainably offer above 3.5% in this environment.

If the Fed cuts further in 2026, bank savings rates will follow.

That mechanism is not the same as a bank holding your money in T-bills. It is driven by borrower demand in DeFi lending markets, which does not move in lockstep with US monetary policy.
